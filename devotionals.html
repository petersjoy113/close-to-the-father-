On Peace

When Stillness Feels Impossible

"Be still, and know that I am God: I will be exalted among the heathen, I will be exalted in the earth."

— Psalm 46:10, KJV

There is a particular kind of exhaustion that comes not from doing too much, but from worrying too much. It is the fatigue of the mind that never rests, the heart that never stops rehearsing what could go wrong, the spirit that keeps reaching for control over things it was never meant to hold. If you have been living in that place lately, this verse was written for you.

"Be still." Two words that are far easier to read than they are to live. We live in a world that rewards motion. Busy people are valued people. Stillness can feel like laziness, like giving up, like refusing to fight for what matters. But the stillness God calls us to here is not passivity — it is a profound act of trust. It is the decision to stop striving long enough to remember who is actually in charge.

What Stillness Really Means

In the Hebrew, the word translated "be still" carries the idea of releasing, letting drop, letting go. Like a hand that has been gripping something so tightly the knuckles have gone white — and finally, deliberately, opens. God is not asking you to pretend the storm does not exist. He is asking you to release your white-knuckle grip on the outcome.

Notice what follows the command: "and know that I am God." The stillness is not an end in itself. It is the doorway to knowing — really knowing, not just believing in the abstract — that God is who He says He is. When we are in constant motion, constantly talking, constantly planning and adjusting and worrying, we cannot hear. Stillness makes room for knowing.

A Prayer for the Restless Heart

If you find yourself reading this in a season of inner noise, try this today: take one minute — just sixty seconds — and sit with this verse. Not to analyse it. Not to turn it into a sermon. Simply to let it land. Say the words slowly: "Be still. And know that I am God." Let the knowing come not through your intellect but through your breath, your posture, your willingness to stop for just a moment.

God does not need your stillness to be perfect. He does not need an hour of silent contemplation. He meets us in the sixty seconds we actually give Him. Start there. He will meet you there.

On Strength

Strength That Does Not Come From You

"I can do all things through Christ which strengtheneth me."

— Philippians 4:13, KJV

This is one of the most quoted verses in the Bible, and perhaps one of the most misunderstood. It appears on gym walls and sports jerseys, in motivational speeches and graduation cards. And while there is nothing wrong with drawing courage from it, there is something important we lose when we remove it from its context.

Paul did not write Philippians 4:13 from a place of triumph. He wrote it from prison. He wrote it after describing a life that included poverty and abundance, fullness and hunger, peace and suffering. The verse is not a promise that you can achieve anything you set your mind to. It is a testimony that you can endure anything you are called to walk through — because the One walking with you is stronger than whatever you are facing.

The Difference Between Achievement and Endurance

Our culture celebrates achievement. We want the breakthrough, the promotion, the answered prayer, the miracle. And God does give those things. But the deeper gift Paul is describing here is something quieter and rarer: the ability to remain steady, faithful, and even joyful in seasons when the breakthrough has not come yet.

"Christ which strengtheneth me" — the word strengtheneth in the original Greek carries the idea of being poured into, being infused with power from the outside. Paul is not describing inner resolve or human willpower. He is describing a strength that arrives from somewhere else entirely, that flows into you precisely at the moment your own strength runs out.

Where to Go When You Are Empty

If you are facing something today that feels beyond you — a relationship that is breaking, a diagnosis that is frightening, a loss that has hollowed you out, a task that seems impossible — hear what this verse is really saying. It is not telling you to try harder. It is telling you to lean further. The strength you need is not already inside you waiting to be unlocked. It is in Christ, and it flows to you through connection with Him.

Pray not "give me the strength to do this" but "strengthen me — come into this with me." The difference is subtle but everything. One asks for a resource. The other invites a presence.

On Hope

The God Who Plans Your Future

"For I know the thoughts that I think toward you, saith the Lord, thoughts of peace, and not of evil, to give you an expected end."

— Jeremiah 29:11, KJV

Before we open this verse, we need to sit with where it was first spoken. God gave these words to the prophet Jeremiah to deliver to the Israelites — but not in a season of blessing and ease. He delivered them while His people were in captivity in Babylon. They had lost their homes, their temple, their freedom. From every natural vantage point, the future looked like nothing but more of the same suffering.

Into that darkness, God says: I know the thoughts I think toward you. Not "I knew." Not "I will think." Present tense, active tense — right now, in the middle of your captivity, I am thinking about you. My thoughts toward you are of peace. My plans for you are toward an expected end — a future and a hope.

When You Cannot See the Future

One of the most disorienting experiences in the Christian life is the season when you genuinely cannot see what is ahead. The path that seemed clear has become obscured. The plan you had for your life has fallen apart. The thing you were certain God was doing seems to have stalled or disappeared entirely. In those seasons, Jeremiah 29:11 can feel almost cruel if read too quickly — like an empty platitude offered to real pain.

But read slowly, it is the most stabilising truth in scripture. It does not say you will understand the path. It does not say the captivity will be short. What it says is that there is a mind at work on your behalf — a mind that is not confused, not anxious, not scrambling for solutions. God knows the thoughts He thinks toward you. He has not forgotten you in your wilderness. He is thinking about you right now.

Hope as an Anchor, Not a Feeling

Biblical hope is not optimism. Optimism is a feeling that things will probably turn out all right. Hope is a settled conviction anchored in who God is, regardless of how things feel in the moment. You can have hope on the worst day of your life because hope does not depend on your circumstances — it depends on the character of the God who holds your future.

Today, if your future feels uncertain, bring it to this verse. Not to receive an explanation, but to receive an anchor. His thoughts toward you are good. His plans for you are toward a future. Rest in that — not because you can see it, but because He can.

On Faith

Walking When You Cannot See

"For we walk by faith, not by sight."

— 2 Corinthians 5:7, KJV

Seven words. Short enough to memorise in thirty seconds, deep enough to spend a lifetime learning. Paul was not describing a spiritual ideal when he wrote this — he was describing the actual mechanics of the Christian life. Walking by faith is not the advanced course reserved for mature believers. It is the basic mode of operation for anyone who follows Jesus. The path forward is almost never fully visible.

Think about what walking actually requires. Each step is a small act of trust — your foot leaves the ground, commits to the air, and lands somewhere your full weight has not tested yet. We do this thousands of times a day without thinking, because we trust the ground will be there. Faith works the same way, except what we are trusting is not visible ground but an invisible God whose track record of faithfulness is the only evidence we carry.

What Sight Would Actually Give You

We tend to think that if we could only see the full picture — know how the situation will resolve, understand why God is allowing this particular difficulty, see the end from the beginning — we would find it easier to trust. But that is not how it works. Sight would give you information. It would not give you relationship.

Faith is the currency of relationship with God. When a child trusts a parent without understanding the parent's reasons, that trust is not weakness — it is love operating at its most fundamental level. God invites us into this kind of trust not because He enjoys keeping us in the dark, but because the act of trusting Him when we cannot see is precisely what deepens our knowledge of who He is.

The Next Step Is Enough

You do not need to see the whole staircase. You only need light for the next step. If you are standing at a point today where you genuinely cannot see what comes next — where the decision ahead of you is unclear, where the outcome of what you are trusting God for is hidden — this verse is your permission to move anyway.

Walk. Not because you can see, but because you know who is walking with you. That is faith. And faith, Scripture promises, is never wasted.

On Forgiveness

The Weight You Were Not Meant to Carry

"And be ye kind one to another, tenderhearted, forgiving one another, even as God for Christ's sake hath forgiven you."

— Ephesians 4:32, KJV

Forgiveness is one of the most misunderstood concepts in the Christian faith. We confuse it with reconciliation — but they are not the same thing. We confuse it with trust — but forgiveness does not automatically restore trust. We confuse it with saying what was done to us was acceptable — but forgiveness and accountability are not opposites. And perhaps most dangerously, we confuse it with a feeling — and then despair when we pray for it and the feeling does not come.

Forgiveness, at its core, is a decision. It is the decision to release another person from the debt they owe you — not because they deserve it, not because what they did was small, but because you yourself have been forgiven of a debt you could not pay. The standard Paul sets in this verse is uncomfortable: forgive as God for Christ's sake has forgiven you.

What God's Forgiveness Actually Cost

To understand what we are being asked to extend, we first need to understand what has been extended to us. God's forgiveness was not cheap. It was not simply a decision made from a comfortable distance. It required the cross — the most costly act in human history — to make our forgiveness possible. The weight of what we owed was not dismissed; it was paid. By someone else. For our sake.

This is the foundation from which all Christian forgiveness flows. We forgive not because we are naturally generous or because the offence was small, but because we stand in the ongoing experience of a forgiveness that cost everything. When that reality is alive in us, extending forgiveness — even painful, costly forgiveness — becomes less like a moral achievement and more like a natural overflow.

Forgiveness as Freedom

Here is what nobody tells you about unforgiveness: it costs you more than it costs the person you are withholding it from. Bitterness is a burden. Resentment is exhausting. The mental energy spent rehearsing an offence, imagining confrontations, nursing a wound — all of that belongs to you, not to the person who hurt you. Forgiveness does not let them off the hook. It takes you off the hook.

If you are carrying something today that has been heavy for too long — an offence, a betrayal, a wound that will not close — bring it to God first. You do not have to manufacture the feeling of forgiveness. Ask Him to do in you what you cannot do on your own. He is very good at that.
